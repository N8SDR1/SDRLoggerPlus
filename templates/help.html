Overview

SDRLogger+ is a browser-based ham radio contact logger designed for use with Expert Electronics TCI/SDR compatible rigs (EESDR, Anan, Hermes-Lite transceivers) via the TCI (Transceiver Control Interface) protocol, with full waterfall integration. It provides real-time frequency, mode and callsign synchronisation between your radio and the logging form, a live DX cluster display, distance mapping, ADIF import/export, and solar propagation data — all in a single dark-themed interface. SDRLogger+ also supports flrig (W1HKJ) for clean XML-RPC rig control with bidirectional frequency/mode control, and any HamLib-compatible rig via rigctld — all three rig paths can run simultaneously with TCI taking priority. CW keying is supported via TCI (Thetis), K1EL WinKeyer (USB/serial), or HamLib (rigctld).

SDRLogger+ also features automatic digital mode QSO logging — QSOs completed in WSJT-X, JTDX, MSHV, VarAC, and other digital applications are captured automatically over UDP or TCP and saved to your log without any manual entry. Completed QSOs can be automatically uploaded in real-time to QRZ Logbook, ARRL LoTW, Club Log, and eQSL.cc. An integrated Rotator Control panel can command your antenna rotator via PstRotator, GS-232, or EasyComm II whenever a DX station is looked up, automatically pointing your beam at the short-path or long-path bearing. The built-in CW Keyer / Decoder popup lets you send CW text via TCI or HamLib, decode incoming CW from TCI audio or any sound device, and fire programmable macro buttons with live {CALL}, {NAME}, and {RST} substitution from the QSO entry form.

First Run Setup

1 — Configure Your Station

Click the ⚙ Settings button in the top-right corner and fill in:

  • My Callsign — your callsign (e.g. N8SDR). This is sent to the DX cluster as your login and displayed in the header.
  • Operator Name — your first name, used for cluster login prompts.
  • Grid Square — your Maidenhead locator (e.g. EN82). Required for the distance map and beam headings.
Your callsign must be set before connecting to any DX cluster.

2 — TCI / Thetis Connection

Enter your Thetis TCI host (default 127.0.0.1) and port (default 50001). When connected, the TCI LIVE badge lights up in the header and the logging form auto-fills frequency, mode, and spotted callsigns.

SDRLogger+ sends rig control commands back to Thetis via TCI — selecting a Band from the QSO entry panel QSYs Thetis to that band's center frequency, and selecting a Mode sends the corresponding modulation change. Digital log modes (FT8, FT4, JS8, DATA, WSPR, VarAC, PSK31, etc.) are automatically mapped to DIGU (Digital Upper) before being sent — Thetis does not use software mode names directly.

If you change the TCI host or port in Settings and click Save Settings, SDRLogger+ automatically drops and re-establishes the TCI connection — no restart required.

3 — Callsign Lookup (QRZ or HamQTH)

SDRLogger+ supports two callsign lookup services. Configure one or both — QRZ is tried first, with HamQTH used automatically as a fallback if QRZ is not configured or the callsign is not found.

QRZ.com

Enter your QRZ.com username and password. An active QRZ XML Data subscription is required for full lookups. When a callsign is entered, SDRLogger+ automatically fills the Name and QTH fields and updates the distance map.

To upload each saved QSO automatically to your QRZ online logbook, enter your QRZ Logbook API Key (found under qrz.com → My Logbook → Settings → API Key). The Auto-upload QSOs to QRZ Logbook checkbox controls whether uploads fire — it is ON by default. Uncheck it to disable uploads while keeping your key saved.

HamQTH.com

HamQTH.com offers a free callsign lookup service — no subscription required. Register for a free account at hamqth.com, then enter your username and password in Settings → HamQTH Lookup. Click Test Login to verify your credentials.

HamQTH returns name, QTH, grid square, country, CQ zone, and ITU zone — the same fields used to fill the QSO entry form and update the distance map.

If you have neither QRZ nor HamQTH configured, the callsign lookup button and auto-fill will not activate. HamQTH is recommended for operators who do not have an active QRZ XML subscription.

ARRL LoTW (Logbook of the World)

SDRLogger+ can automatically upload each saved QSO to ARRL Logbook of the World — the ARRL's QSO confirmation and award credit system used by operators worldwide.

LoTW uses digital certificate signing rather than an API key. Uploads are handled by the TQSL (TrustedQSL) application which must be installed separately and configured with your LoTW callsign certificate before SDRLogger+ can upload on your behalf. To set up:

  1. Install TQSL from lotw.arrl.org and complete the LoTW account and certificate request process
  2. In TQSL, create a Station Location (File → Station Locations) — give it a memorable name like Home-QTH
  3. In SDRLogger+ ⚙ Settings → LoTW, enter the full path to tqsl.exe (typically C:\Program Files (x86)\TrustedQSL\bin\tqsl.exe), or just tqsl if TQSL is on your Windows system PATH
  4. Enter your Station Location name exactly as it appears in TQSL. If left blank, SDRLogger+ falls back to your callsign certificate
  5. Click 🔍 Verify TQSL Path to confirm SDRLogger+ can find the executable
  6. Check Auto-upload QSOs to LoTW and click Save Settings

Once enabled, each QSO is written to a temporary ADIF file and passed to TQSL with the flags -d -u -x -l <Station Location>, which signs the record with your certificate and uploads it to LoTW in the background. The QSO save confirmation message shows LoTW ✓ on success or a brief error if the upload fails. For full technical details on the submission protocol see the LoTW developer documentation ↗.

Club Log

SDRLogger+ can upload each QSO in real-time to Club Log — the popular online tool for DXCC tracking, contest logging, and log verification used by operators worldwide.

Club Log uses its own credential system — enter your Club Log email and an Application Password (generated at clublog.org → Settings → App Passwords). The application API key is built into SDRLogger+ — you do not need to obtain or enter one.

Optionally set a Callsign to upload under (leave blank to use your station callsign) and an Upload Designator — a single letter (default B) added to uploaded records so they are not duplicated on future uploads.

The 🔑 Test Credentials button uses Club Log's safe getlotwstate.php endpoint to verify your email, password, and callsign without uploading any data — this will not trigger IP blocking.

⚠ Important — IP blocking: Club Log automatically firewalls IP addresses that send repeated failed authentication requests to its real-time upload endpoint. SDRLogger+ implements a one-strike rule: if a single 403 error is received during a real-time upload, all further uploads are immediately disabled until you correct your credentials in Settings and re-test. This protects your IP from being blocked.
LoTW upload is off by default. It only activates once you have checked Auto-upload QSOs to LoTW in Settings and saved. QRZ Logbook and LoTW uploads are independent — you can enable both, either, or neither.

PATH note: If you enter just tqsl as the path (without a full directory), TQSL must be added to your Windows Environment Variables → Path (System Properties → Advanced → Environment Variables). If you use the full path to tqsl.exe this is not required.

eQSL.cc

SDRLogger+ can upload each QSO in real-time to eQSL.cc — the free electronic QSL card exchange system used by operators worldwide.

Setup is simple — just a username and password:

  1. Create a free account at eqsl.cc if you don't already have one
  2. In SDRLogger+ ⚙ Settings → eQSL.cc, enter your eQSL username and password
  3. Click 🔑 Test Credentials to confirm they are accepted
  4. Check Auto-upload QSOs to eQSL.cc and click Save Settings

Each saved QSO is uploaded immediately in ADIF format. The ADIF header includes PROGRAMID:10>SDRLogger+ which suppresses the "Logger not found" notice on the eQSL site. Digital mode QSOs logged via WSJT-X, JTDX, MSHV, or VarAC are also uploaded automatically.

eQSL does not block IP addresses on repeated requests, so there is no risk of being locked out. The save toast notification will show · eQSL ✓ on a successful upload.

flrig Rig Control

flrig (by W1HKJ, author of fldigi) is a standalone transceiver control application that exposes an XML-RPC server on your local network. SDRLogger+ connects to it as a client for frequency/mode following, DX spot tuning, and bidirectional control — changing the band or mode in SDRLogger+ pushes the change to the radio via flrig, and changes on the radio update SDRLogger+ automatically.

flrig is the recommended rig control option for most users. It has a straightforward GUI for rig setup, supports all major Icom, Yaesu, Kenwood, and Elecraft transceivers, and provides the cleanest integration path of all available rig control options.

How It Works

flrig runs as a small GUI application on your Windows PC. It handles the direct serial/USB connection to your transceiver and exposes all rig functions over an XML-RPC interface on localhost:12345 (default). SDRLogger+ polls this interface every 1.5 seconds for frequency and mode updates, and sends tune commands when you click a DX spot.

Priority order for rig control: TCI → flrig → HamLib. All three can be enabled simultaneously — each acts as a fallback for the one before it. If you run TCI for your Expert Electronics SDR and flrig for a second radio, both work at the same time without conflict.

Step 1 — Install flrig

Download the latest Windows installer from W1HKJ's site: w1hkj.com/files/flrig/ ↗. Run the installer — no command-line setup required.

Step 2 — Configure Your Rig in flrig

  1. Open flrig and go to Config → Setup → Transceiver
  2. Select your rig from the Rig dropdown
  3. Select the correct COM port (check Device Manager → Ports if unsure)
  4. Set the Baud Rate to match your radio's CAT/CI-V menu setting
  5. Click Init — flrig should show your rig's frequency and mode in its window
For Icom radios: set CI-V Transceive ON in the rig's menu (SET → Connectors → CI-V) so flrig receives frequency updates automatically when you turn the VFO.

Step 3 — Enable in SDRLogger+

  1. Open ⚙ Settings → flrig
  2. Check Enable flrig rig control
  3. Leave Host as 127.0.0.1 and Port as 12345 unless you changed flrig's XML-RPC port
  4. Click Test Connection — should return your rig name and current frequency
  5. Click Save Settings

Once connected the FLRIG: <rig name> badge appears in the header (blue). Frequency and mode follow automatically. Clicking a DX cluster spot tunes the rig instantly. Changing the band or mode selector in SDRLogger+ pushes the change back to the radio.

Digital Mode Name (Icom / Yaesu / Kenwood)

When clicking a DX cluster spot for FT8, FT4, JS8, WSPR, or PSK31, SDRLogger+ needs to tell flrig which audio-passthrough mode name to use. Different manufacturers use different names:

RadioMode Name to Enter

Set this in Settings → flrig → Digital Mode Name. Leave blank to let SDRLogger+ auto-detect (may not work on all rigs).

RTTY Mode Name — a separate field for RTTY cluster spots. Leave blank for native FSK RTTY (default). Set to USB-D if you run RTTY via fldigi in AFSK mode, which routes audio through the USB-D passthrough just like FT8.

HamLib Rig Control

SDRLogger+ can connect to any HamLib-compatible transceiver via the rigctld network daemon — giving non-TCI rig owners full frequency/mode following and DX spot tuning. Over 300 radios are supported including Yaesu FT-991A, FT-DX10, Icom IC-7300, IC-7610, IC-705, Kenwood TS-590, TS-890, and many more.

How It Works

HamLib's rigctld program runs on your PC and acts as a small TCP server on port 4532. SDRLogger+ connects to it as a client, polling for frequency and mode updates every 750 ms and sending tune commands when you click a DX spot.

TCI and HamLib can both be enabled at the same time. TCI always takes priority — HamLib only updates the form and tunes the rig when TCI is not connected. This lets TCI SDR users enable HamLib as a fallback without any conflict.

Step 1 — Install HamLib

Download the Windows binary package from the official HamLib download page: github.com/Hamlib/Hamlib/wiki/Download ↗ (direct Windows builds also available at SourceForge ↗). Extract the zip anywhere — no installer needed. The key executable is rigctld.exe.

Step 2 — Find Your Rig Model Number

Run the following in a command prompt to list all supported rigs and find your model number:

rigctl.exe -l

Common model numbers:

Step 3 — Start rigctld

Open a command prompt and run your rigctld command, for example:

rigctld.exe -m 3073 -r COM3 -s 19200 -T 127.0.0.1 -t 4532

Leave this window open — rigctld must keep running while SDRLogger+ is in use. You can create a .bat file to launch it automatically alongside SDRLogger+.

Step 4 — Enable in SDRLogger+

  1. Go to ⚙ Settings → HamLib / rigctld
  2. Check Enable HamLib rig control
  3. Set Host to 127.0.0.1 and Port to 4532 (defaults are correct if rigctld is on the same PC)
  4. Click Test Connection to verify — a green confirmation message confirms rigctld is reachable
  5. Click Save Settings

Once connected, the RIG LIVE badge appears in the header. Frequency and mode will begin following your rig, and clicking any DX cluster spot will tune your radio automatically.

K1EL WinKeyer — Hardware CW Keyer

SDRLogger+ can connect to a K1EL WinKeyer (WK1, WK2, WK3, WKmini, or compatible clone) via USB/serial for dedicated hardware CW keying. This is the recommended CW option for operators who don't use TCI — the WinKeyer handles all CW timing, weighting, and PTT control in hardware, independent of PC timing jitter.

How It Works

SDRLogger+ speaks the K1EL WinKeyer host protocol at 1200 baud, 8-N-2 (8 data bits, no parity, 2 stop bits). When you type text in the CW Keyer panel and click SEND, SDRLogger+ sends raw ASCII characters to the WinKeyer over the serial port. The WinKeyer converts them to perfectly timed CW and keys your transmitter via its Key and PTT output jacks.

A background thread monitors the connection and automatically reconnects if the WinKeyer is unplugged and re-plugged, or if the USB port resets.

CW priority order: TCI → WinKeyer → HamLib. If TCI is connected, CW always routes through Thetis. WinKeyer is used when TCI is not connected. HamLib's \send_morse is the last fallback.

Step 1 — Connect the WinKeyer

  1. Plug your WinKeyer into a USB port. Windows assigns a COM port (e.g. COM2).
  2. If you don't know the COM port, check Device Manager → Ports (COM & LPT).
  3. Connect the WinKeyer's Key output to your radio's CW key jack, and optionally the PTT output to your radio's PTT jack.

Step 2 — Enable in SDRLogger+

  1. Go to ⚙ Settings → K1EL WinKeyer
  2. Check Enable WinKeyer
  3. Select your COM port from the dropdown (SDRLogger+ auto-detects available ports)
  4. Set your preferred defaults:
    • Default WPM — initial sending speed (overridden by the CW panel slider)
    • Key Output — Port 1 (default), Port 2, or Both
    • Keyer Mode — Iambic B (default), Iambic A, Ultimatic, or Bug (for paddle input)
    • Enable PTT — check if your WinKeyer drives a PTT line to the radio
    • PTT Lead / Tail — delay before first element / after last element (ms)
  5. Click Test WinKeyer — a confirmation shows the firmware version (e.g. ✓ WinKeyer v31 on COM2)
  6. Click Save Settings

Once connected, the CW Keyer popup shows a WinKeyer badge in the top-right corner, and the TX path label shows via WinKeyer.

DXCC Entity Lookup & Worked Before

When you enter a callsign (or click a spot), SDRLogger+ resolves the DXCC entity using a built-in country database (cty.dat). The entity name, continent, CQ zone, and ITU zone are displayed below the callsign field.

A colour-coded Worked Before badge also appears:

  • ★ NEW ENTITY — you have never worked any station in this DXCC entity before.
  • NEW BAND/MODE — you have worked this entity, but not on the current band + mode combination.
  • No badge — you have already worked this entity on this band and mode.

The ⚠ Worked Before banner also appears with a list of previous QSO dates, bands, and modes if you have any prior contacts with that specific callsign.

Click QRZ to open a detailed lookup popup for the entered callsign.

QSO Log

The centre panel shows all logged contacts. Use the search box in the panel header to filter by callsign, band, mode, remarks, or contest/event name in real time.

  • Click ✏ (white pencil) to edit any field of a saved QSO.
  • Click 🗑 (red trash) to delete — a confirmation prompt will appear.
  • Use ◀ Prev and Next ▶ buttons above the table to page through your log. Page size adjusts automatically based on the panel's visible height.
  • Resize the panel vertically by dragging the resize handle at the bottom edge — the page size recalculates when you resize.
  • Right-click any log entry to open a context menu with a 📡 Spot option — spot that contact directly from the log within a configurable time window after the QSO was worked.

DX Cluster

TCILog supports up to 3 simultaneous Telnet DX cluster connections plus the Spothole.app REST API aggregator (POTA, SOTA, RBN, clusters — no API key required). All sources are merged into a single spot list.

Connecting

Configure cluster servers in Settings (DX Clusters section), then click CONNECT in the DX Cluster panel. The status dot turns green when connected.

Spot List Controls

  • Band filter — automatically tracks your TCI VFO frequency and filters to the active band.
  • Age filter — dropdown (5/10/15/30 min) prunes old spots.
  • Max spots — number input (1–100) limits how many spots are displayed at once (default 25).

How CW is Keyed

SDRLogger+ routes CW through the best available path automatically, in this priority order:

  1. TCI connected — sends cw_macros:0,text directly to Thetis via the TCI WebSocket. Thetis handles all timing and keys the radio hardware (HL2, ANAN, Hermes-Lite) via its internal CWX engine. No COM port or PTT interface needed.
  2. WinKeyer connected — sends ASCII text to a K1EL WinKeyer (or compatible clone) via USB/serial at 1200 baud, 8-N-2. The WinKeyer handles all CW timing, weighting, and PTT control in hardware. Configure the COM port, keyer mode (Iambic A/B, Ultimatic, Bug), key output port, and PTT lead/tail in Settings. The badge shows WinKeyer when connected.
  3. HamLib connected — sends \send_morse text to rigctld, which keys the rig via CAT/serial.
flrig does not support CW keying — the flrig XML-RPC API has no send_morse method. This is by design: flrig handles frequency/mode/PTT only. For CW without Thetis/TCI, use WinKeyer or HamLib.
Break-in (QSK) is not a TCI command — it is configured directly in Thetis Setup → CW and applies to all CW transmitted through Thetis regardless of source.

CW Decoder

The decoder uses IQ downconversion — sample-by-sample quadrature mixing — to track the carrier envelope at up to ~1400 updates per second. A running matched filter (0.3× dit, or 0.55× with DSP enabled) smooths sub-dit noise spikes with minimal edge smearing; its bandwidth adapts automatically and can be tightened further with the DSP toggle for conventional rigs. An optional Noise Blanker (NB) clamps impulse noise before detection — useful for rigs without SDR-grade filtering. Detection uses two gates in series: Squelch gates the decoder off entirely until the signal-to-noise ratio is sufficient; within that gate Threshold sets the exact mark/space boundary with proportional hysteresis that scales to the threshold level, preventing chatter on marginal signals. A lookback edge buffer filters noise blips (marks <20% of dit) and threshold flicker (spaces <8% of dit) by merging them into surrounding edges — this prevents noise spikes from splitting inter-character spaces and causing text to run together. Each element is classified by a Bayesian Gaussian timing model that learns the dit and dah distributions for the specific operator being copied — no fixed ratio is assumed. The model bootstraps from the first 5 elements (using your TX WPM as the starting estimate) then switches to full Bayesian classification. Speed tracking is asymmetric: the model follows speed-ups quickly and slowdowns more cautiously, matching the way real operators accelerate. Inter-element, inter-character, and word spacing are all tracked independently with separated clamp ranges to prevent model collapse — this means Farnsworth-spaced CW (fast elements with stretched gaps) is decoded correctly without run-together or chopped text. The AFC includes multi-signal rejection: when two CW signals are close together in the passband, it holds steady on the locked signal instead of jumping between them. The peak tracker has QSB/fading recovery and word-gap re-snap — after a deep fade or long word space, the decoder snaps its peak level back instantly when the signal returns. A noise floor holdoff freezes the floor after each mark→space transition so the envelope decay tail doesn't contaminate the noise reference. All timing uses sample-based counting for jitter-free edge measurements. Pressing CLR clears decoded text but intentionally preserves the learned timing so the decoder does not have to re-adapt to the same station. Decoded text appears in the RX Decode window. Both windows are resizable — drag the bottom edge.

Quick Start — Getting a Decode

  1. Select your audio source from Audio In and click Start Listening
  2. Watch the waterfall — a CW signal appears as a bright vertical streak. If you see one, click it to tune the decoder to that frequency (⌖ Snap mode will lock to the peak automatically)
  3. Confirm the Sig bar pulses on each dit and dah. If it does not, the Tone Hz or AFC is not centred on the signal — click the streak in the waterfall again
  4. The first few characters use a timing bootstrap; full Bayesian decoding kicks in after ~5 elements and accuracy improves from there
  5. If copy looks good but speed is wrong, enable Auto-match RX WPM and set reasonable min/max clamps (e.g. 10–40 WPM)

Troubleshooting Decodes

  • Sig bar flat / no output — Squelch may be too high (lower it toward ×1.1), or the tone frequency is off. Look at the waterfall: the signal streak should be under the yellow cursor line. Click the streak to re-tune.
  • Garbage characters / noise triggers — Squelch too low or Threshold too low. Raise Squelch first; if the band is noisy, try ×2.0–×2.5. Then raise Threshold if false marks persist — start around 40–50% and work up; on a very noisy band values of 60–75% may be needed. The slider goes to 90%.
  • Decoding stops after running for a while — click the signal in the waterfall to force a full reset and immediate re-calibration. This is equivalent to a Stop/Start but without losing your audio device selection.
  • Changed frequency and decoding died — move the Tone slider to the new sidetone pitch (or click the new signal in the waterfall). The full signal chain resets automatically on any tone change.
  • Dits read as dahs or vice versa — the model is still bootstrapping. Give it a full callsign exchange (~10–15 elements) to adapt. Ensure TX WPM is set close to the station's actual speed before you start listening.
  • AFC jumps to wrong signal — the AFC now has multi-signal rejection (requires 3 consecutive scans on a new frequency before jumping), but if two strong signals are very close it may still switch. Disable AFC, click the correct signal in the waterfall using ⌖ Snap mode, then re-enable AFC. Or switch to ⌖ Exact click mode and click precisely on the desired carrier.

  • Waterfall — a scrolling spectrum display sits to the right of the two control columns (Tone/AFC on the left, Squelch/Threshold on the right), showing ±400 Hz around the set tone. New rows appear at the top; colour runs from black (no signal) through dark blue to white (strong signal). A red cursor line marks the live AFC-locked (or manually set) frequency with a Hz label. Frequency scale ticks appear along the bottom of the strip. The ⌖ Snap / ⌖ Exact toggle sits directly to the right of the canvas. Works with both TCI and microphone audio — no separate data source needed.
    • Click to tune — click anywhere on the waterfall while listening to re-tune the decoder to that frequency. A full signal-chain reset happens automatically so the new frequency starts calibrating immediately.
    • ⌖ Snap (default) — click mode that finds the strongest signal peak within ±60 Hz of your click and locks onto it. Useful when a carrier is visible in the waterfall but not exactly under your cursor.
    • ⌖ Exact — click mode that tunes to precisely where you click. Toggle between Snap and Exact using the ⌖ Snap / ⌖ Exact button; your preference is saved.
    • The waterfall is purely a display aid — it has no effect on decode quality and can be ignored entirely if not needed.
  • Squelch — sets the minimum signal-to-noise ratio required before the decoder runs at all. The decoder measures how much stronger the detected tone is relative to the background noise floor and only activates when that ratio meets this threshold. Range is ×1.1 to ×3.0, default ×1.5. Lower values (×1.1–×1.3) allow the decoder to work on weak or noisy signals but increase the chance of false triggers from band noise. Higher values (×2.0–×3.0) require a strong clean signal before decoding starts, which reduces garbage output on a busy or noisy band. Start at ×1.5 and adjust: lower if weak signals are not triggering the decoder at all; raise if noise is producing random characters when no signal is present.
  • Threshold — sets how far above the noise floor the signal must be to register as a mark. Slide left to lower the threshold (more sensitive — decodes weaker signals, but more noise risk). Slide right to raise it (more selective — strong signals only, cleaner output). Default 35%; range 15–90%. A proportional hysteresis system scales with the threshold level, and a debounce filter rejects noise blips shorter than 20% of a dit — this prevents brief noise spikes from splitting inter-character spaces and causing text to run together.
  • DSP toggle — enables a tighter matched-filter bandwidth (0.85× dit instead of 0.5×) for improved noise rejection. Off by default for SDR users — Thetis already provides a narrow CW filter (500 Hz) and NR2/NR3 noise reduction. Enable for conventional rigs (IC-9100, FT-991A, TS-590, etc.) where the IF passband may be 1200 Hz or wider and no DSP noise reduction is available. The tighter filter narrows the effective decoder bandwidth to match the CW element rate, significantly improving weak-signal copy on rigs without SDR-grade filtering.
  • NB toggle — enables an impulse noise blanker that clamps static crashes and key clicks before they reach the envelope detector. Tracks a running average of signal magnitude and limits any spike exceeding 3.5× the average. Off by default for SDR users — Thetis NR2/NR3 handles impulse noise in the SDR processing chain. Enable for conventional rigs whose built-in noise blanker is insufficient or unavailable.
  • Sig bar — shows real-time signal strength at the detected tone frequency. Should pulse clearly on each dit and dah. If the bar is completely flat while a signal is audible, the tone frequency is off — use the waterfall to click the correct signal.
  • RX WPM display — shows the decoder's current speed estimate (e.g. 22 WPM), updated after every classified element. Useful for confirming the model has locked onto the correct timing. Shows — WPM before any elements have been classified.
Recommended starting settings for most signals: Squelch ×1.5 · Threshold 35% · AFC ON · Snap mode ON. Lower Squelch toward ×1.1 for weak signals; raise toward ×2.5 for a noisy band. On a clean band, Threshold rarely needs to move off 35%. On a noisy band, raise Squelch first, then nudge Threshold up in small steps — the slider goes up to 90% for high-QRM conditions.

Auto-Match RX WPM

When enabled, the TX WPM slider automatically tracks the speed of the station you are copying:

  • Inside the editor, the token buttons at the top ({MYCALL}, {MYNAME}, {CALL}, {NAME}, {RST}, {NR}) insert the token at the cursor position — click any token button to insert it wherever your cursor is in the text field
  • Press Enter or click OK to save; press Esc or click outside the modal to cancel
  • Macro text and labels are saved automatically in your browser
  • Click RESET in the Macros panel header to restore all 16 buttons to factory defaults (confirmation required)

Contest Serial Number ({NR})

The {NR} token inserts the current serial number, zero-padded to three digits (001, 002 … 999, 1000 …). It is designed for simple CW contests where an incrementing exchange number is required.

  • Enable the NR {NR} toggle in the Send panel to activate contest mode. When OFF, the token still expands but nothing increments and nothing is logged.
  • The current serial is displayed in the NR {NR} row; use + / − to manually adjust; CLR resets to 1 (with confirmation)
  • When the toggle is ON and any macro or typed message containing {NR} is sent successfully, the serial automatically increments to the next number
  • The sent serial number is appended to the Remarks field in the QSO entry panel (e.g. NR: 007) so it is logged with the contact
  • The serial number is saved to disk and survives restarts — you can close SDRLogger+ and resume a contest later without losing your count
  • A typical contest NR macro: NR {NR} {NR} TU DE {MYCALL} K

DX Spotting

SDRLogger+ can send DX spots directly from the logging form or from a recent log entry. Configure spotting under ⚙ Settings → DX Clusters → DX Spot Sending.

Spot Destination

A three-way segmented toggle controls where spots are sent. Only one destination is active at a time:

SettingWhat it does

Confirm Before Spot

When Confirm before spot is checked (default ON), clicking the Spot button shows a one-line confirmation strip below the button row displaying the callsign, frequency, mode, and destination. Click SEND to transmit or Cancel to abort. Uncheck this option for single-click spotting during fast pileup or contest operating.

Spotting from the Entry Form

While a callsign is entered in the logging form, the 📡 Spot button uses the current form data — callsign, frequency, and mode. This is the primary path for spotting a station you are working or hearing.

Spotting the Last Logged QSO

After clicking ▶ Save QSO the form clears, but SDRLogger+ retains the just-logged callsign, frequency, and mode in a short-term buffer. The Spot button changes to 📡 Spot (W1AW) indicating it will spot the last logged station. This window is open for 10 minutes after logging — after that the buffer expires and the button returns to its idle state.

This means you can log a QSO first and spot it immediately after without re-entering any data.

Spotting from the Log Table

Right-click any row in the QSO log to access the context menu. The 📡 Spot this QSO option is available for QSOs logged within the last 10 minutes.

  • Within 10 minutes — option is active and clickable, respects Confirm setting
  • Older than 10 minutes — option is shown greyed out with the age displayed (e.g. "Spot — window expired (23 min ago)")
  • Destination set to OFF — option shows "enable destination in Settings"
The 10-minute limit on log-row spotting is fixed and cannot be changed. A DX spot is a real-time "this station is on the air now" signal — spotting old QSO data would send inaccurate information to the cluster network.

Backup & Restore

SDRLogger+ stores all QSOs in two local SQLite database files: hamlog.db for general logging and pota.db for POTA activations. The Backup & Restore tools in ⚙ Settings → Backup & Restore operate on whichever database is currently active (based on your GENERAL / 🏕 POTA mode toggle).

Database Backup

Click Backup DB to save a timestamped copy of your QSO database (hamlog_backup_YYYYMMDD_HHMMSS.db).

  • If a Database Backup Folder path is configured (e.g. C:\Radio\Backups), the file is written directly to that folder and the full saved path is confirmed on screen.
  • If the folder field is left blank, the file downloads through your browser instead.
Set a backup folder path and click Save Settings first — then every future backup goes straight to that folder with one click.

Database Restore

Click Restore DB and pick a previously backed-up .db file. Before overwriting anything, SDRLogger+ automatically saves a safety copy of the current database (hamlog_pre_restore_YYYYMMDD_HHMMSS.db or pota_pre_restore_... in POTA mode) to your backup folder (or alongside the active database if no folder is configured). A ✅ Restore complete confirmation appears and the log panel reloads automatically.

Restore replaces your entire active QSO database. The automatic safety backup means you can always undo — but verify you are in the correct mode (General vs POTA) before restoring.

Delete Database

The ⚠ Danger Zone at the bottom of Backup & Restore provides separate Delete General DB and Delete POTA DB buttons. Clicking either requires two confirmation dialogs to prevent accidents. Before deletion, a timestamped safety backup is automatically saved to the backups folder (e.g. general_pre_delete_20260412_143000.db). After deletion, a fresh empty database is created in its place.

This permanently removes all QSO records from the selected database. The safety backup can be restored using Restore DB if needed.

Settings Export & Import

Your SDRLogger+ configuration (callsign, QRZ credentials, cluster servers, rotator settings, display preferences, etc.) is stored in your browser's local storage. To back it up or move it to a different browser or machine:

  • Export Settings — downloads hamlog_settings_YYYYMMDD_HH.json containing all your preferences.
  • Import Settings — pick a previously exported .json file. All settings are restored and the page reloads automatically.
Export your settings after initial setup and save the file alongside your database backups. If your browser cache is ever cleared or you switch to a new PC, import the file and you are back up immediately — no re-entering QRZ keys or cluster servers.

Click the 📡 Spot Myself button in the activation banner to announce your activation to the POTA.app ↗ network so hunters can find you on the cluster. This uses the official POTA API and requires a free POTA.app account.

🛰 SAT Mode — CSN Technologies S.A.T. Integration

SDRLogger+ provides deep, real-time integration with the CSN Technologies S.A.T. satellite controller (firmware by K. Bush AC2MI). When running alongside the S.A.T., SDRLogger+ becomes a complete satellite logging environment with live tracking, Doppler-corrected frequencies, and seamless QSO push — all over your local network with zero additional hardware.

SAT mode is a third option on the General / 🏕 POTA / 🛰 SAT mode toggle in the header. Switch to SAT mode to transform the entire UI for satellite operating.

How It Works

SDRLogger+ communicates with the S.A.T. controller over four complementary network channels — all standards-based:

ChannelProtocolPurpose
/track APIHTTP GET (polled every 2s)Live AZ/EL, Doppler frequencies, transponders, GPS, timing
UDP 9932UDP broadcastEvent notifications: AOS, LOS, START TRACK, TRANSPONDER, QSO, STOP
QSO LOG TYPEADIF over UDP (port 1100)Real-time QSO records pushed when you log on the S.A.T.
/adif endpointHTTP GET (on demand)Bulk fetch of stored QSO log with duplicate detection

Setup

1. Configure SDRLogger+

Open ⚙ Settings → 🛰 Satellite tab and enter:

FieldValue
Enable S.A.T. Integration✅ checked
S.A.T. IP AddressYour S.A.T.'s IP (e.g. 192.168.200.194)
UDP Broadcast Port9932 (default)
QSO Log Port (ADIF)1100 (default)

2. Configure the S.A.T.

On the S.A.T. web UI → LOCATION section, set:

  • QSO LOG TYPE: ACLog (or N1MM, Log4OM, HRD — all send standard ADIF)
  • QSO LOG IP: Your PC's IP address
  • QSO LOG PORT: 1100 (must match SDRLogger+ "QSO Log Port")

3. Open Windows Firewall

In an Administrator command prompt, run these two commands:

netsh advfirewall firewall add rule name="SDRLogger+ SAT UDP" dir=in action=allow protocol=UDP localport=9932
netsh advfirewall firewall add rule name="SDRLogger+ SAT ADIF" dir=in action=allow protocol=UDP localport=1100

4. Switch to SAT Mode

Click the 🛰 SAT button in the General / 🏕 POTA / 🛰 SAT toggle at the top of SDRLogger+. The Satellite Status panel appears in place of the DX Cluster, and live data populates within seconds once the S.A.T. is tracking a bird.

The Satellite Status Panel

Replaces the DX Cluster panel when in SAT mode. Refreshes every 2 seconds from the S.A.T.'s /track API.

Satellite Section

  • Satellite name and NORAD catalog number
  • Status: IDLE / TRACKING / AOS — PASS ACTIVE / LOS
  • AZ / EL: real-time azimuth and elevation (green when above horizon)
  • Max EL: peak elevation for the current pass
  • Range: distance to the satellite in kilometers
  • AOS Az / LOS Az: azimuth at acquisition and loss of signal
  • Time to LOS: live countdown (turns red under 60 seconds)
  • Elapsed: time since AOS

Transponder Section

  • Active transponder name and mode
  • Uplink / Downlink in MHz, Doppler-corrected from the S.A.T.
  • Full transponder list showing every available transponder for the tracked satellite

Pass QSO Log

Real-time list of QSOs logged during the current pass — from any source (SDRLogger+ form or S.A.T. device).

Connection Bar

  • S.A.T. Live indicator with grid square, GPS sat count, and ADIF port
  • ⬇ Fetch S.A.T. Log button — pulls the entire stored QSO log from the S.A.T. with duplicate detection

Real-Time QSO Push

When you log a QSO on the S.A.T. device itself (using its built-in LOG ENTRY screen), the S.A.T. broadcasts an ADIF record over UDP to SDRLogger+. The QSO appears in your log within ~2 seconds with a toast notification: 🛰 SAT QSO received from S.A.T.

This works with any of the S.A.T.'s QSO LOG TYPE options (N1MM, DXKeeper, HRD, Log4OM, ACLog, MacLogger) — they all send standard ADIF over UDP.

"⬇ Fetch S.A.T. Log" Button

The S.A.T. controller stores up to 100 QSO log entries internally. The ⬇ Fetch S.A.T. Log button (in the connection bar of the Satellite Status panel) pulls the entire stored log directly from the S.A.T. via its /adif endpoint.

Smart duplicate detection (callsign + date + time-to-the-minute) ensures no double entries — you can hit the button anytime without worry.

Use this when:

  • SDRLogger+ wasn't running during a session and QSOs are sitting on the S.A.T.
  • You want to verify nothing was missed after a pass
  • Before clearing the S.A.T.'s internal log

ADIF Export & LoTW

SAT QSOs are exported with full ADIF 3.1 satellite fields for LoTW, Club Log, eQSL, and other services:

  • PROP_MODE: SAT — required for satellite DXCC credit
  • SAT_NAME — satellite name (e.g. SO-50, ISS, RS-44)
  • FREQ — uplink frequency
  • FREQ_RX — downlink frequency (LoTW satellite credit)
  • BAND / BAND_RX — derived from frequencies
  • All standard QSO fields: CALL, GRIDSQUARE, MODE, RST_SENT, RST_RCVD, QSO_DATE, TIME_ON, COMMENT

Troubleshooting SAT Mode

No data appears in the Satellite Status panel

  • Verify the S.A.T. IP address in ⚙ Settings → 🛰 Satellite is reachable from your PC (try opening http://<sat-ip> in a browser)
  • Confirm the S.A.T. is powered on and tracking a satellite
  • Make sure the firewall rules above are in place

QSOs logged on the S.A.T. don't appear in SDRLogger+

  • Confirm QSO LOG IP on the S.A.T. matches your PC's IP
  • Confirm QSO LOG PORT matches the SDRLogger+ "QSO Log Port" (default 1100)
  • Add the firewall rule for port 1100 (see Setup step 3)
  • As a fallback, click ⬇ Fetch S.A.T. Log to pull QSOs manually

The S.A.T. badge isn't showing

  • The gold S.A.T. badge only appears in SAT mode (it stays hidden in General and POTA modes by design)
  • Confirm "Enable S.A.T. Integration" is checked in Settings

Performance & What's Counted

All four trackers are powered by server-side worked caches built once at application startup and incrementally updated as new QSOs are logged. This means:

  • Opening the Awards Dashboard is instant, even with thousands of QSOs
  • Filtering and sorting happens in the browser with no server round-trips
  • New QSOs appear in the dashboard the next time you reload it

All four trackers count QSOs from your General log database (hamlog.db). POTA contacts (in pota.db) and SAT contacts (in hamlog.db with prop_mode='SAT') are also counted toward DXCC, WAS, WAZ, and WPX awards — they're just regular QSOs as far as the awards are concerned.
